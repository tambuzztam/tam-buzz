## The Principle

"Prize the problem" means to cherish it, to embrace it, to take it on with a will as your own. The right problem prized will be the same thing as treating your own growth as of the utmost importance.

## Why Prize Problems?

Philosophical problems are not obstacles to overcome or symptoms to eliminate. They are:

- **Tied to who we are**: Problems emerge from the intersection of your values, experiences, and the world you encounter
- **Connected to who we must become**: They point toward necessary growth and development
- **Rooted in where we've come from**: They reflect the wisdom and experience you've accumulated

## The Growth Connection

When you prize a problem, you recognize it as a **rich harvest**—something to add to your treasure store as:

- A great goad (motivation for growth)
- An encouragement (validation of your engagement with what matters)
- A guide (direction for your development)
- An enhancement of ongoing growth

## What This Looks Like

Prizing the problem means:

- Moving from "How do I get rid of this?" to "What is this teaching me?"
- Seeing confusion as the beginning of clarity, not an enemy of it
- Understanding that the right problem will activate your natural response to what's important
- Treating philosophical difficulties as invitations to grow into more of yourself

## In Practice

You don't need to arrive already having identified and formulated a problem. Discovery and problem-finding is integral to our process. But when a problem emerges—when you hear yourself articulating what's truly puzzling or concerning you—that's cause for gladness.

The problem becomes a companion in your philosophical journey, not a burden to be carried.
